# Language acquisition — key terms & concepts

> Working glossary for language acquisition theory. To be expanded as new concepts surface. Initially seeded from Morg's narrow reading article and Krashen's research.

## Core hypotheses (Krashen)

**Input Hypothesis** — Language is acquired (not learned) through exposure to **comprehensible input** — messages slightly above the learner's current level (i+1). Production (speaking/writing) emerges naturally after enough input.

**Affective Filter Hypothesis** — Stress, anxiety, and low motivation raise a mental "filter" that blocks input from being processed. Low-stress, enjoyable environments = lower filter = better acquisition.

**Monitor Hypothesis** — Conscious grammar knowledge serves only as an "editor" (monitor) for output. Fluency comes from acquired (subconscious) knowledge, not learned rules.

**Natural Order Hypothesis** — Grammatical structures are acquired in a predictable order, regardless of teaching order. Some structures just take longer.

## Reading-specific concepts

**Narrow Reading** (Krashen, 1981) — Reading texts by one author or about a single topic of interest. Ensures natural repetition of vocabulary and grammar within a constrained domain. Counter-intuitively recommended especially for beginners.

**First Few Pages Effect** — The first ~15–20% of any new book/author is disproportionately difficult due to unfamiliar writing style, vocabulary, and narrative patterns. After this hurdle, reading smooths out significantly. Switching material resets this progress.

**Reader Fatigue** — Cumulative cognitive load from constantly adapting to new authors, topics, and vocabulary sets. Narrow reading minimizes this.

**Contextual Reading** — Comprehension aided by pre-existing familiarity with the topic, characters, setting, or domain (beyond just language knowledge). You understand because you already know what to expect.

**Content Words vs Function Words:**
- **Content words** carry meaning: nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs. Domain-specific, vary drastically by topic.
- **Function words** (grammatical words) provide structure: particles, auxiliary verbs, pronouns, conjunctions. Stable across all topics.

The beginner's main reading barrier is the volume of unknown **content words**. Narrow reading constrains this; jumping topics explodes it.

**Graded Readers** — Simplified texts written for language learners at specific proficiency levels. Controlled vocabulary and grammar. Useful transition tool but often too artificial to sustain motivation.

## Tools & techniques

**SRS (Spaced Repetition System)** — Algorithm-driven flashcard review (e.g., Anki) that schedules reviews at optimal intervals for long-term retention. Most effective for vocabulary, less so for grammar.

**Immersion** — Extended exposure to native material (reading, listening, watching) without focusing on explicit study. The core engine of acquisition.

**i+1** — Input at a level just above the learner's current competence. Hard enough to stretch, easy enough to follow. The sweet spot for acquisition.

## Other terms from the article

**Collocations** — Words that frequently appear together (e.g., "heavy rain" vs "strong rain"). Acquired naturally through repeated exposure in context rather than deliberate study.

**Affective Filter** — See Affective Filter Hypothesis above. The emotional/attitudinal barrier that blocks input processing.
