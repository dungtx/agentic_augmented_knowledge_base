# Enterprise Architecture

Understanding the customer's organizational structure to navigate the buying process effectively. This is about **stakeholder mapping**, not technical architecture.

## Key Stakeholder Functions in ITO Decisions

| Function | Role in Decision | What They Care About |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **C-Level / Executive** | Budget authority, strategic alignment | ROI, competitive advantage, risk at company level |
| **Finance / Procurement** | Contract terms, payment schedules | Price, payment terms, liability clauses, audit rights |
| **IT / Engineering Leadership** | Technical evaluation, vendor capability | Technical competence, security, scalability |
| **HR** | Headcount planning, team structure | Cost-per-head, time-to-fill, retention |
| **Legal** | Contract review, IP protection | Liability caps, IP ownership, data handling, termination clauses |
| **Operations / PMO** | Project governance, reporting | Delivery methodology, status reporting, escalation paths |

## Buying Committee Dynamics

Most ITO decisions involve multiple stakeholders. Map:

- **Sponsor** — Champions the deal internally, helps navigate
- **Decision Maker** — Has formal authority to sign
- **Influencers** — Technical evaluators, end users, procurement
- **Blockers** — Can veto or delay; often Legal, Security, or skeptical middle management

## Stakeholder Management Tactics

- **Early**: Identify all stakeholders; don't rely on a single point of contact
- **Often**: Different stakeholders need different conversations (see [[Customer Persona]])
- **Always**: Address blockers directly — ignoring them doesn't make them go away

## Notes

- In enterprise ITO, Procurement and Legal can derail deals at the last minute. Engage them early, not just at contract time.
- Security/Compliance teams are increasingly involved — have ISO 27001, SOC 2, or relevant certifications ready.
- HR involvement often signals staff augmentation deals; absence of HR suggests project-based work.
